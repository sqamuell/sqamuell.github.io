Poetry & Rationality

The push and pull of the two sides of my brain is a topic I’ve thought about a lot, mostly in relation to the dueling personalities of my parents (my mother: the right brain, my father: the left). For a long time, I believed that I was a left-brain person, a cold, analytical thinker who made decisions based on probabilities and logic. At the time, if you asked me what I was learning in architecture school, I might’ve told you that I was really starting to use my right brain for the design of buildings. But soon after, I began to realize that the belief that I was a left-brain person allowed for the neglectance of empathy and compassion. This pattern is something that I’ve started to notice permeates American society. “My statistic is higher valued than your experience.” The dominating left-brain is made ever more popular with our reliance on data and the continued saturation of western thought. To ignore its other half is to fall into the trap.

Does That Make Sense? (2021)

 The fact that we need breaks from our computer screen lends some starting insight: western society has an enormous reliance on visual representation. We know this. The better a design appears in images and representation, the more sure the buyer or client can be of its success. We live to get and we get what we see. The visual is the portrayal of a concrete, unmoving, illustrated entity. It finds itself nearly everywhere in 21st century life. We, especially, find it extremely relevant in the realm of architecture. The most ‘complete’ architecture allows no room for interpretation and, instead, has entire clarity of the designer’s intent. The common misconception that an architect is the “framer” of their work highlights this fallacy. Leaving no room for interpretation of use and meaning, the imagination of the user has no relevancy, it excludes them. Anticipation of needs is often misplaced due to a misunderstanding ingrained in the ego of the architect. Though a design may be well intended, the impact will always vary person to person.

--

 Judith Butler, in her book Frames of War, claims that the choice and composition of the framing of photography tells us as much about the photographer as it does the subject of the photo. I think this is so interesting. And I think this concept transcends photography. Our mind’s eye is constantly painting imagery, thought, and emotion related to our interpretation. Though the architect, photographer, or poet may mean one thing, the overlaying of our perception - our “framing” - will cause it to mean something else entirely.

 It’s impossible to communicate anything fully. In the process of transmission and reception, the smallest differences in our fundamental understandings of a topic produce different results. In fact, you’re doing it right now. We literally can’t not interpret things differently than our friend and neighbor. Our mind finds a way to fit new information in with the old and rationalizes connections while doing so. This presentation is being interpreted in as many different ways as there are individuals listening. We do share some common understanding. This overlap, though comforting, is quite boring. The clashing of different understanding is what defines us as separate individuals. I am my experiences and actions, and they are me. Nothing I think or draw or say or do can every truly be separate. My being is not limited to the ends of my body.

Horizon

The sky met the horizon
And the line drew a boundary
Skepticism only benefited others
And the ducks followed
The lady with the bread
Thesky meets the horizon
And there’s so much in between
Skepticism benefits no one but myself
And I follow
The lady with the bread
--

 The interpretable inconsistency I’m discussing is as prevalent in architecture and the visual arts as it is the written ones. It’s just harder to see, masked behind centuries of conditioning and belief. I believe the drastic difference of our emotional takeaways of a string of words and letters is actually common ground. The acceptance that everything has these moments of complete disconnection is not a bad thing. Western culture sees disconnection as an opportunity to learn and benefit itself. Western culture claims empathy when in means complete understanding. It misunderstands the value of compassion. I used to misunderstand—and I'm stil learning—the value of compassion.
